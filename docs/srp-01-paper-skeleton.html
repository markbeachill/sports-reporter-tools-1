Important Because It Isn't: Paper Skeleton — SRP-01

Paper skeleton: argument, gap, outline and references

A working scaffold for a theoretical article on the reader's journey in sports reporting, attachment under economy, and the limits of automation

#1. Working title and thesis

Working title: “Important because it isn't: triggering reader attachment under economy, and what automated match reports cannot do.”

One-sentence thesis: Because sport is, in the worldly sense, without material consequence, the match report's task is not to inform but to grant the reader permission to feel fully and safely; this is achieved by triggering pre-existing affective dispositions through extreme economy of language, and it is precisely this — not accuracy — that automated reporting cannot do.

#2. Gap statement (the paragraph the reviewer looks for)

Affective disposition theory explains sports enjoyment as a product of the reader's dispositions toward competitors and the outcome (Zillmann, Bryant and Sapolsky, 1989; Raney, 2006), and schema-triggered affect explains how those dispositions can form rapidly, even toward unknown figures, from cues in a text (Fiske, 1982; Raney, 2004; Kinnally et al., 2013). Separately, a sociology of play locates sport within a “magic circle” set apart from ordinary life, where freedom is possible precisely because no material interest is at stake (Huizinga, 1949; Caillois, 1961). And a small empirical literature shows that readers find human-written match reports more enjoyable than automated ones even when they rate the two equally accurate (Clerwall, 2014; Graefe and Bohlken, 2020; Duncan, Kunert and Karg, 2025). Each of these literatures is, however, developed for a different object than the one studied here. Disposition and schema research is built on the live spectator and the long feature, where attachment can be BUILT over time; play theory is largely silent on journalism; and the automation studies measure THAT an enjoyment gap exists without theorising WHY it falls where it does, or WHERE in a text it is made and lost. This article addresses the under-examined case at their intersection: the match report, a perishable form of a few hundred words in which a writer cannot build disposition but must TRIGGER it under severe economy, and in which the value created is not information but sanctioned feeling. It argues that this reframing both explains the measured human-automation gap and specifies the craft the gap consists of.

#3. Section outline

#1. Introduction — the puzzle

Open on the measured anomaly: readers enjoy human reports more but rate them no more accurate (Duncan et al., 2025). Pose the question the measuring studies leave open — why, and where in the writing? State thesis. Read: Duncan et al. (2025); Clerwall (2014).

#2. What the report is for — important because it isn't

The ontology section. Sport as magic circle: free because without material interest. Crucially, use the LEAKY version — Huizinga's own ambivalence, and the ludological critique — to set up the bias argument later. The report's value is sanctioned feeling, not information. Read: Huizinga (1949); Caillois (1961); Consalvo (2009).

#3. How feeling is produced — disposition and schema

The mechanism section. Disposition theory (enjoyment = disposition × outcome) and the counter-intuitive suspense result (affection HEIGHTENS the pain of possible loss — “it's the hope that kills you”). Then schema-triggered affect: dispositions can be triggered fast, from cues, even toward the unknown. Read: Zillmann, Bryant and Sapolsky (1989); Zillmann (1996); Raney (2004, 2006); Fiske (1982); Kinnally et al. (2013).

#4. The constraint the literature skips — economy

The contribution's hinge. The feature BUILDS disposition; the report must TRIGGER it in a few hundred words. Develop “economy”: the lever-pull, the reader-supplied weight (“1935”), the misallocation failure (budget spent on frame, bankrupt before the moment). This is where the paper goes beyond all cited work. Read: Kinnally et al. (2013) for the unknown-character case; the rest is the author's craft argument.

#5. Why automation fails here specifically

Tie ontology + mechanism + economy to the empirical gap. Automation clears accuracy (quality ties) and fails at sanctioned feeling (enjoyment diverges) because it hands a database, which cannot open the safe arena. Distinguish the HONEST failure of template systems from the predicted DISHONEST failure of LLMs (imitating levers without lived context). Read: Duncan et al. (2025); Graefe and Bohlken (2020); Thurman, Dörr and Kunert (2017).

#6. Bias, freedom and the leak

The ethics/discourse section, and a genuine sub-contribution. Partisanship is the circle exercising freedom and is harmless WITHIN it; it becomes harmful only where the circle leaks into consequence (race, real harm). This explains why bias is lifeblood in sport and poison in consequential journalism — same fact, different tethering. Read: Sapolsky (1980) on race-as-predictor; the leak argument is the author's, grounded in Huizinga's own anti-separation.

#7. An instrument and a programme (future work)

Introduce SR1 as an operationalisation — a three-track (factual / attachment / economy) reader-journey analyser — and report the matched-pair convergence with Duncan et al. AS PRELIMINARY, foregrounding the validity limits (self-marking, priming, untested LLM-adversary). Present the cold re-test protocol as the proposed programme. Do NOT overclaim the instrument. This is the bridge to a second, empirical paper.

#8. Conclusion

Restate: the match report's craft is granting safe, full feeling under economy; this is what automation cannot do and what the analyst can be taught to see. The meaninglessness of sport is not the limit of the form's seriousness but the source of its freedom.

#Theory to treat with caution (cite as the frame being moved beyond)

Ruggiero, T.E. (2000) ‘Uses and gratifications theory in the 21st century’, Mass Communication and Society, 3(1), pp. 3–37.

Note: uses and gratifications is included so the paper can position itself against it, not lean on it. Its list of needs is post-hoc and effectively unfalsifiable; it explains the demand side weakly and says nothing about how prose evokes feeling. Use it to mark what the disposition/economy account does better.
